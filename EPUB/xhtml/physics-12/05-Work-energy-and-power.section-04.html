Power

Now that we understand the relationship between work and energy, we are ready to look at a quantity that defines how long it takes for a certain amount of work to be done. For example, a mother pushing a trolley full of groceries can take 30 \text{30}~ s \text{s} or 60 \text{60}~ s \text{s} to push the trolley down an aisle. She does the same amount of work, but takes a different length of time. We use the idea of power to describe the rate at which work is done.

The unit watt is named after Scottish inventor and engineer James Watt (19 January 1736 - 19 August 1819) whose improvements to the steam engine were fundamental to the Industrial Revolution. A key feature of it was that it brought the engine out of the remote coal fields into factories.

Power

Power is defined as the rate at which work is done or the rate at which energy is expended. The mathematical definition for power is:

P = F · v P=F·v

uid129 is easily derived from the definition of work. We know that:

W = F · d . W=F·d.

However, power is defined as the rate at which work is done. Therefore,

P = Δ W Δ t . P=\frac{\Delta W}{\Delta t}.

This can be written as:

P = Δ W Δ t = Δ F · d Δ t = F Δ d Δ t = F · v \begin{align*} P& = \frac{\Delta W}{\Delta t}\\ & = \frac{\Delta \left(F·d\right)}{\Delta t}\\ & = F\frac{\Delta d}{\Delta t}\\ & = F·v \end{align*}

The unit of power is watt (symbol W).

Historically, the horsepower (symbol hp) was the unit used to describe the power delivered by a machine. One horsepower is equivalent to approximately 750 \text{750}~ W \text{W}. The horsepower was derived by James Watt to give an indication of the power of his steam engine in terms of the power of a horse, which was what most people used to for example, turn a mill wheel.

Machines are designed and built to do work on objects. All machines usually have a power rating. The power rating indicates the rate at which that machine can do work upon other objects.

A car engine is an example of a machine which is given a power rating. The power rating relates to how rapidly the car can accelerate. Suppose that a 50 kW engine could accelerate the car from 0 \text{0}~ km·hr^{-1} \text{km·hr^{-1}} to 60 \text{60}~ km·hr^{-1} \text{km·hr^{-1}} in 16 \text{16}~ s \text{s}. Then a car with four times the power rating (i.e. 200 \text{200}~ kW \text{kW}) could do the same amount of work in a quarter of the time. That is, a 200 \text{200}~ kW \text{kW} engine could accelerate the same car from 0 \text{0}~ km·hr^{-1} \text{km·hr^{-1}} to 60 \text{60}~ km·hr^{-1} \text{km·hr^{-1}} in 4 \text{4}~ s \text{s}.

A forklift lifts a crate of mass 100 \text{100}~ kg \text{kg} at a constant velocity to a height of 8 \text{8}~ m \text{m} over a time of 4 \text{4}~ s \text{s}. The forklift then holds the crate in place for 20 \text{20}~ s \text{s}. Calculate how much power the forklift exerts in lifting the crate? How much power does the forklift exert in holding the crate in place?

Determine what is given and what is required

We are given:

  • mass of crate: m= 100 \text{100}~ kg \text{kg}

  • height that crate is raised: h= 8 \text{8}~ m \text{m}

  • time taken to raise crate: t r = 4   s {t}_{r}=4 \text{s}

  • time that crate is held in place: t s = 20   s {t}_{s}=20 \text{s}

We are required to calculate the power exerted.

Determine how to approach the problem

We can use:

P = F Δ x Δ t P=F\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}

to calculate power. The force required to raise the crate is equal to the weight of the crate.

Calculate the power required to raise the crate
P = F Δ x Δ t = m · g Δ x Δ t = 100   kg 9 , 8   m · s − 2 8   m 4   s = 1960   W \begin{align*} P& = F\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}\\ & = m·g\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}\\ & = \left(100 \text{kg}\right)\left(9,8 \text{m}·{\text{s}}^{-2}\right)\frac{8 \mathrm{m}}{4 \text{s}}\\ & = 1960 \text{W} \end{align*}
Calculate the power required to hold the crate in place

While the crate is being held in place, there is no displacement. This means there is no work done on the crate and therefore there is no power exerted.

Write the final answer

1960 \text{1960}~ W \text{W} of power is exerted to raise the crate and no power is exerted to hold the crate in place.

Simple measurements of human power

You can perform various physical activities, for example lifting measured weights or climbing a flight of stairs to estimate your output power, using a stop watch. Note: the human body is not very efficient in these activities, so your actual power will be much greater than estimated here.
