Chapter summary

Presentation: VPmmk

  1. Ohm's Law governs the relationship between current and potential difference for a circuit element at constant temperature. Mathematically we write I = V R I=\frac{V}{R}.

  2. Conductors that obey Ohm's Law are called ohmic conductors; those that do not are called non-ohmic conductors.

  3. Ohm's Law can be applied to a single circuit element or the circuit as a whole (if the components are ohmic).

  4. The equivalent resistance of resistors in series ( R s {R}_{s}) can be calculated as follows: R s = R 1 + R 2 + R 3 + . . . + R n {R}_{s}={R}_{\text{1}}+{R}_{\text{2}}+{R}_{\text{3}}+...+{R}_{n}

  5. The equivalent resistance of resistors in parallel ( R p {R}_{p}) can be calculated as follows: 1 R p = 1 R 1 + 1 R 2 + 1 R 3 + . . . + 1 R n \frac{\text{1}}{{R}_{p}}=\frac{\text{1}}{{R}_{\text{1}}}+\frac{\text{1}}{{R}_{\text{2}}}+\frac{\text{1}}{{R}_{\text{3}}}+...+\frac{\text{1}}{{R}_{n}}

  6. Real batteries have an internal resistance.

  7. The potential difference V V of the battery is related to its emf E \mathcal{E} and internal resistance r r by:

    E = V load + V internal resistance or E = I R E x t + I r \begin{align*} \mathcal{E}& = V_{\text{load}} + V_{\text{internal resistance}}\\ &\text{or} \\ \mathcal{E}& = IR_{Ext} + Ir \end{align*}

  8. The external resistance in the circuit is referred to as the load.

Recall that current is the movement of electric charge from a higher potential to a lower potential.

Potential difference, or voltage is a way to describe the difference in electrical potential energy across a component in a circuit. Remember that a resistor converts electrical potential energy into thermal energy, so the electrical potential is higher on the "in" side compared to the "out" side of a resistor.

Ohm's law describes the relationship between the total current   I   ~I~ through an ohmic conductor, its resistance R R, and the potential difference V   V~ across it:

I = V R           (3) I = \frac{V}{R}~~~~~\textrm{(3)}

For the equivalent circuit, we can rewrite (3) \textrm{(3)} as:

I A = V A R e q           (4) I_A = \frac{V_A}{R_{eq}}~~~~~\textrm{(4)}

We are asked to calculate the potential difference V 1 V_{1} over R 1 R_{1}, as shown in the circuit diagram above, so we use Ohm's law and rewrite (3) \textrm{(3)} to get:

V 1 = I 1 R 1           (5) V_1 = I_1R_1~~~~~\textrm{(5)}

Since the circuit in question is a series circuit, we know that the current I 1 I_{1} flowing through R 1 R_{1}, is the same as the current I A I_{A}. This is represented as:

I 1 = I A           (6) I_1 = I_A~~~~~\textrm{(6)}

Substituting (6) \textrm{(6)} into (5) \textrm{(5)} we get:

V 1 = I A R 1           (7) V_1 = I_AR_1~~~~~\textrm{(7)}

Substituting (4) \textrm{(4)} into (7) \textrm{(7)} we get:
