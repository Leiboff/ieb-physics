Momentum and Newton’s Second Law

Newton’s Second Law of Motion can be defined in terms of momentum.

Newton’s Second Law can be written as

F n e t = m a F_{net}=m a

Where 

F n e t = resultant force (N) m = mass (kg) a = acceleration (m.s − 2 ) \begin{align} F_{net} &= \text{resultant force (N)} \\ m &= \text{mass (kg)} \\ a &= \text{acceleration (m.s}^{-2}\text{)}\end{align} 

But acceleration is the rate of change of velocity and can be written as a = Δ v Δ t a=\frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}

Combining these equations 

F n e t = m Δ v Δ t F_{net} =m \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}

So for constant mass

F n e t = Δ p Δ t F_{net} = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}

This is an alternative form of Newton’s second law.

Impulse

When a resultant force acts on an object for a period of time the object will undergo a change in velocity.  Momentum which is the product of mass and velocity must therefore also change.  The change in momentum is directly proportional to the resultant force and to the time for which the force acts.

Starting with Newton's second law,

F n e t = Δ p Δ t F_{net}=\frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}

Rearranging:

F n e t Δ t = Δ p F_{net} \Delta t = \Delta p

The left hand side of this equation is a measure of the strength of the force and the duration for which the force acts. This combination is defined as  impulse.

Definition

The principle of conservation of momentum: The total momentum for a system of 

interacting objects (objects colliding or exploding apart) remains constant,  providing that 

there are no external forces acting on the system.

Note: Before an explosion, the total momentum is zero if nothing is moving.  After an 

explosion, the total momentum is still zero.
