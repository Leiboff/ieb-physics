Chapter summary

Presentation: 243S

  • Ohm's Law states that the amount of current through a conductor, at constant temperature, is proportional to the voltage across the resistor. Mathematically we write I = V R I = \frac{V}{R}

  • Conductors that obey Ohm's Law are called ohmic conductors; those that do not are called non-ohmic conductors.

  • We use Ohm's Law to calculate the resistance of a resistor. R = V I R = \frac{V}{I}

  • The equivalent resistance of resistors in series ( R s R_{s}) can be calculated as follows: R s = R 1 + R 2 + R 3 + … + R n R_{s} = R_{1} + R_{2} + R_{3} + \ldots + R_{n}

  • The equivalent resistance of resistors in parallel ( R p R_{p}) can be calculated as follows: 1 R p = 1 R 1 + 1 R 2 + 1 R 3 + … + 1 R n \frac{1}{R_{p}} = \frac{1}{R_{1}} + \frac{1}{R_{2}} + \frac{1}{R_{3}} + \ldots + \frac{1}{R_{n}}

  • Electrical power is the rate at which electrical energy is converted in an electric circuit.

  • The electrical power dissipated in a circuit element or device is P = V I P=VI and can also be written as P = I 2 R P=I^2R or P = V 2 R P=\frac{V^2}{R} and is measured in joules (J).

  • The electrical energy dissipated is E = P t E=Pt and is measured in joules (J).

  • One kilowatt hour refers to the use of one kilowatt of power for one hour.

1 R p = 1 R 1 + 1 R 2 = 1 50 + 1 50 = 2 50 R p = 25  Ω \begin{align*} \frac{1}{R_{p}} & = \frac{1}{R_{1}} + \frac{1}{R_{2}} \\ & = \frac{1}{50} + \frac{1}{50} \\ & = \frac{2}{50} \\ R_{p} & = \text{25}\text{ Ω} \end{align*} V = I R = ( 0,09 ) ( 25 ) = 2,25  V \begin{align*} V & = IR \\ & = (\text{0,09})(\text{25}) \\ & = \text{2,25}\text{ V} \end{align*}

Electric Eels

Electric eels have a series of cells from head to tail. When the cells are activated by a nerve impulse, a potential difference is created from head to tail. A healthy electric eel can produce a potential difference of 600 \text{600}~ V \text{V}.

What is meant by “a potential difference of 600 \text{600}~ V \text{V}”?

Potential difference or voltage, V V, is the amount of energy per unit charge needed to move that charge between two points in a circuit. In the example of the electric eel this is the amount of energy per unit charge needed to move that charge from the head to the tail.

How much energy is transferred when an electron is moved through a potential difference of 600 \text{600}~ V \text{V}?

An electron has a charge of − 1,69 × 10 − 19 -\text{1,69} \times \text{10}^{-\text{19}}~ C \text{C}. So we can find the amount of energy needed to move this charge through the potential difference:

V = E C 600 = E − 1,69 × 10 − 19 E = − 1,014 × 10 − 16  J \begin{align*} V & = \frac{E}{C} \\ \text{600} & = \frac{E}{-\text{1,69} \times \text{10}^{-\text{19}}} \\ E & = -\text{1,014} \times \text{10}^{-\text{16}}\text{ J} \end{align*}
